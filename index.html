1) Rail signalling principles: fail‑safe, track circuits, CBTC

What to knowclick to toggle
  • Fail‑safe design: systems default to the safest state on fault (e.g., a failed track circuit reads occupied so signals hold at stop).
  • Track circuits: rails form an electrical circuit; train axles shunt the circuit to indicate occupancy. Broken rail/insulation failure usually → occupied (safe fail).
  • Interlocking: prevents conflicting routes from being set at the same time.
  • Fixed vs moving block: conventional signalling divides the line into fixed blocks; CBTC uses continuous comms and precise train location to allow dynamic moving blocks → higher capacity.
  • CBTC building blocks: ATP (protection), ATO (operation), ATS (supervision).
Interview talking pointsclick to toggle
  • Give a 60‑second example of fail‑safe thinking you’ve used (watchdogs, redundancy, safe‑state I/O).
  • Explain how track circuits detect trains and why failure is designed to be “occupied”.
  • CBTC in a sentence: continuous radio + precise location + moving block = shorter headways with high safety.
  • Local example: Sydney Metro runs driverless CBTC — highlight benefits.
Cold‑call questionsclick to toggle
  • Why does a track circuit failure cause delays?
  • Outline ATP vs ATO vs ATS in CBTC.

2) Overhead wiring systems: catenary, AC vs DC

What to knowclick to toggle
  • Catenary system: contact wire, catenary/messenger, droppers, insulators, registration arms, masts/gantries, tensioning.
  • Australia: many legacy suburban networks (e.g., Melbourne, Sydney) run 1.5 kV DC; newer heavy rail/freight often 25 kV AC.
  • Why 25 kV AC? Lower current for same power → smaller conductors, wider substation spacing; needs higher clearances & insulation coordination.
  • Interfaces: traction substations step down utility HV and rectify for DC; return current via rails with bonding/earthing to manage stray currents.
Interview talking pointsclick to toggle
  • Give a quick AC vs DC comparison: power losses, substation spacing, clearances, retrofit complexity, EMC/stray current.
  • Point to local examples (1.5 kV DC metros; 25 kV AC mainlines).
  • Mention safety near OHW: isolation permits, earthing, minimum approach distances.
Cold‑call questionsclick to toggle
  • Why is 25 kV AC often chosen for new mainlines?
  • Name the main catenary components you’d see on site.

3) Rail project safety standards: RSNL basics (Australia)

What to knowclick to toggle
  • RSNL establishes the national regulator (ONRSR) and requires accreditation for railway operations.
  • SFAIRP: ensure safety so far as is reasonably practicable by weighing likelihood, severity, knowledge, availability/suitability of controls, and cost.
  • SMS (Safety Management System) is mandatory for accredited operators; contractors must comply with the operator’s SMS.
  • Expectations: hazard ID, change management, competence, fatigue, asset management, assurance, and continual improvement.
Interview talking pointsclick to toggle
  • Explain SFAIRP in a minute and give a design example (e.g., add redundancy where risk is intolerable).
  • How you contribute as a grad: hazard ID, toolbox talks, procedures, incident learning, configuration control.
  • Call out signalling, level crossings, and radio systems as safety‑critical assets requiring documented controls and assurance.
Cold‑call questionsclick to toggle
  • What is SFAIRP and how would you show it on a project?
  • Why is accreditation relevant to contractors and graduates on site?

4) Integration of communication systems in rail

What to knowclick to toggle
  • Train‑to‑wayside comms enable modern control: e.g., GSM‑R‑based DTRS (NSW), ARTC NTCS (carrier networks), FRMCS as GSM‑R successor.
  • Integration points: signalling/interlocking & CBTC, Traffic Management, SCADA (traction/stations), condition monitoring, passenger info/CCTV.
  • Reliability & cybersecurity: redundant bearers, emergency call priority, deterministic interfaces to train control; coverage design in tunnels/cuttings.
Interview talking pointsclick to toggle
  • Sketch how CBTC radios, TMS, and DTRS/NTCS interoperate.
  • Why migrate from GSM‑R to FRMCS and what that implies for spectrum and IP networks.
  • Practicalities: handover, interference management, failover to safe states.
Cold‑call questionsclick to toggle
  • What is the role of DTRS on Sydney’s network?
  • Why are networks moving from GSM‑R to FRMCS?

5) Emerging tech: IoT, predictive maintenance, automation

What to knowclick to toggle
  • IoT & condition monitoring: sensors on rolling stock and track (vibration, temperature, rail stress, WILD) feed predictive models to reduce failures.
  • Digital twins: fuse sensor data with asset models for risk‑based decisions.
  • Automation: CBTC enables driverless/UTO metros; alters safety assurance and maintenance practices.
Interview talking pointsclick to toggle
  • Example: bearing temperature + vibration alerts cutting downtime; WILD + track geometry data to prioritise tamping or welds.
  • Link to SFAIRP: proactive controls reduce risk exposure and verify control effectiveness.
Cold‑call questionsclick to toggle
  • Name two data sources you’d combine for track failure prediction.
  • How does UTO change the safety case and maintenance regime?
